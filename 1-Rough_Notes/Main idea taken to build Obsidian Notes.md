# Obsidian: The King of Learning Tools (FULL GUIDE + SETUP)

The creator uses a Zettelkasten-inspired system in Obsidian to move from raw information to personal insight. His process is divided into a specific folder structure designed to minimize friction and maximize connection (14:00-20:34).

The Folder Structure
Rough Notes (15:52): A catch-all for temporary ideas, reminders, or fleeting thoughts.
Source Material (16:21): Where he stores insights from books, videos, and articles. He notes the page/source and expands on the idea in his own words.
Tags (16:51): Rather than using standard hashtags, he creates empty notes for each tag. This allows him to treat tags as searchable, linkable entities.
Indexes (18:18): When a tag accumulates enough related notes, he converts that tag-note into an index (like a table of contents) to provide structure and easy navigation.
Templates (19:52): Stores a standardized note format (date, status, title, references) to save time (22:30-24:03).
Full Notes / Atomic Notes (20:03): The "workhorse" folder. This is where he writes independent, self-contained mini-essays.
The Writing Process
Capturing: He consumes content and takes notes in the Source Material folder, focusing on rewriting ideas in his own words to ensure understanding (26:00-26:55).
Synthesizing: He creates a new note in the Full Notes folder (27:12). He avoids referencing the original source directly in the body text, preferring to write as if the note is a standalone piece of knowledge (27:17-28:10).
Tagging & Linking: He uses double square brackets [[ ]] to create hyperlinks. He keeps tags focused (4-5 per note) and avoids vague categories (29:21-31:18).
Referencing: Every Full Note ends with a Reference section that links back to the original Source Material and any related notes, creating a web of interconnected ideas (34:36-35:38).
Core Philosophy: He emphasizes keeping notes short (under 500 words) and focused on one single idea to maintain flexibility for future writing projects (33:02-34:17).
